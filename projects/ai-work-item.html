AI-Infused Work Item Experience

Designing intelligent assistance that synthesizes complex work and surfaces clarity at the moment it matters most.

Timeline
October 2025 - Present
Role
Lead designer, research partner
Summary

Work items are where teams coordinate their most critical work—yet when work items grow complex, teams struggle to extract the signal. I led the design of an AI-powered summarization feature that reads the full context of a work item and surfaces a structured, actionable summary: current state, key blockers, prioritized next actions, and open questions.

Through concept testing with 8 users, research validated that teams see AI assistance as a compelling solution to their work item data reliability problem, and that a structured response framework significantly outperforms open-ended AI generation. The work is now ready for rollout, with card covers and other AI-infused experiences enriching the work item view across the platform.

Overview

As Jira moves toward AI-driven product experiences, the work item view presents a critical opportunity: teams rely on work items as their source of truth, yet when work grows complex—multiple subtasks, scattered comments, long histories, attached context—teams struggle to maintain clarity. What's actually happening? What comes next? Who should move it forward?

Rather than asking teams to manually synthesize, I designed an AI assistant that reads the full work item and surfaces what matters. The solution combines research-validated design patterns: a structured response framework that respects cognitive load, surfaces ambiguity, and guides teams toward action.

The Challenge

Work items are the backbone of team coordination in Jira—they hold decisions, dependencies, context, and history. Yet as work grows more complex, they become harder to navigate. A single work item might contain:

  • A multi-paragraph description written weeks ago, now outdated
  • 50+ comments scattered across conversations, decisions, and debate
  • Multiple subtasks with their own nested context
  • Attachments, screenshots, and design files referenced throughout
  • Linked dependencies on other work, some resolved, some blocked

When a teammate opens this work item, the burden falls on them to synthesize: reading through comments in sequence, tracing dependencies, inferring current state from scattered signals. What should happen next? What's actually blocking progress? The work item contains the truth, but the truth is buried.

Teams respond by creating work-arounds: re-asking questions in Slack, keeping parallel notes, maintaining external spreadsheets. All of this is preventable friction—information that already exists in the work item, but requires too much effort to extract.

Work items contain the truth, but as they grow complex, that truth becomes increasingly buried.

Research: Validating the Opportunity

I partnered with a dedicated researcher and product managers to understand how AI could reshape the work item experience. We conducted concept testing with users across business and software product areas, exploring how different AI-first creation experiences and assistance patterns could improve work reliability and clarity.

Research Method

Concept Testing with Figma Prototypes

We built interactive Figma prototypes of different AI-first creation concepts and tested them with 8 users who work actively in Jira. Participants completed representative tasks such as creating new work, exploring existing work, and working through complex scenarios. We gathered both quantitative preference data and qualitative feedback on which patterns felt most natural and useful.

Three Key Findings

Finding 1

Canvas create had the strongest resonance

Users strongly preferred Canvas create (3) over Single create (1) and Multi create (2), and both significantly outperformed current Jira and Monday.com creation experiences. The flexibility and conversational nature felt natural—less like filling out a form, more like discussing the work with someone who understands it.

Finding 2

AI solves a real reliability problem

Teams intend for work items to be a source of truth, but inconsistent behaviours and manual interactions mean information and reliability vary widely. Research confirmed that AI-driven concepts are impressive and solve this pain—users saw AI assistance as a credible way to maintain consistency and completeness without asking teams to change how they work.

Finding 3

Work items evolve, not suddenly complete

Work items typically start as placeholders and get added to/edited over time. This insight shaped the entire design. Rather than fighting this pattern, the solution needed to work with it—offering AI assistance at key moments as the work item grows and changes.

Solution: Structured AI Summarization

Based on research, I designed a feature that helps teams extract clarity from complex work items without adding process. When invoked, the AI assistant reads the complete work item—description, comments, subtasks, attachments, linked items, history—and synthesizes a clear, actionable summary.

How It Works

The Summarization Flow

Users invoke the AI assistant from the work item view. The system reads all available context: the full description, all comments and replies, subtask structure, attachments and files, linked dependencies. It then generates a structured summary designed for scanning and action, following a framework built through research and iteration.

Framework

The AI Response Framework

Research and concept testing revealed that users strongly prefer structured, scannable AI output over open-ended generation. The response framework has four sections:

1. CURRENT STATE
(1-2 sentences)
What is the work? Where are we right now?
2. KEY DECISIONS & BLOCKERS
(Bulleted list)
What decisions have been made? What's blocking progress?
3. NEXT ACTIONS
(3-5 prioritized actions)
What needs to happen next, and who should do it?
4. OPEN QUESTIONS
(Ambiguities that need clarification)
What's unclear or needs more information?
Framework in Context

Adapting to Work Item Complexity

The framework adapts its output based on work item complexity. A sparse work item gets brief, encouraging prompts to add context. A dense, complex work item gets a comprehensive synthesis focusing on recency, blockers, and actionability. The AI reads the current state and responds appropriately—never overwhelming, always actionable.

Why This Framework

Design Rationale

  • Respects cognitive load: Users don't synthesize—the framework does it for them
  • Action-oriented: Moves teams from "I understand" to "I know what to do"
  • Surfaces ambiguity: Open questions flag what needs clarification before proceeding
  • Predictable structure: Users learn where to look for different information types
  • Scales with complexity: Works equally well for 2-comment work items and 50-comment epics

Context: AI Across the Work Item

Beyond summarization, the work item view is gaining complementary AI-infused experiences. Image generation for work items (intelligent card covers), content assistance, and other intelligent features are rolling out across the platform, creating a cohesive AI-first experience. This case study focuses on the summarization feature—a high-impact tool for teams managing complex, long-running work where clarity matters most.

Design Decisions

Why structure matters more than intelligence

Generative AI can produce eloquent, detailed summaries. But research showed that users valued structure and scanability far more than prose quality. A structured framework ensures the summary is predictable, actionable, and works in the context of busy teams who need to move work forward.

During concept testing, we tried multiple response structures. The state → blockers → next steps → open questions sequence consistently won. Other orderings felt less useful and harder to act on. The framework isn't just a design choice—it's research-validated.

Why next actions are explicit, not inferred

Early iterations asked the AI to "suggest next steps." This created ambiguity—suggested by whom? For whom? Required or optional? The framework evolved to "next actions"—making clear that these are concrete, prioritized steps the team should take. Pairing them with ownership (e.g., "Alex needs to resolve the design review before we can proceed") moves the summary from informative to actionable.

Validation & Readiness

Research outcomes validated the direction and readiness for rollout:

What Concept Testing Revealed

  • Framework resonates with users: The structured response pattern was validated across all 8 test participants—users understood it immediately and found it useful.
  • AI solves a real problem: Teams expressed strong intent to use summarization for complex work items, seeing it as a credible solution to their data reliability challenge.
  • Structure beats prose: Structured summaries significantly outperformed open-ended AI output in usefulness ratings and task completion.

What's Next

  • Rollout & iteration: The feature is now being built for production. Initial rollout will measure adoption and gather usage data on which sections users find most valuable.
  • Refine based on real data: Real work items will reveal edge cases and opportunities the framework can evolve to serve better.
  • Integrate across the platform: As other AI experiences roll out (card covers, content assistance), the work item view will become increasingly intelligent and supportive.

Reflection

This work reinforced a core principle: good AI design isn't about impressive generation—it's about removing friction from how teams already work.

The initial temptation was to build a "smarter work item"—more fields to fill, better validation, more guidance. But research showed the problem wasn't a lack of fields; it was that teams were drowning in context. The solution wasn't to ask for more information; it was to synthesize the information that already existed.

The structured response framework also crystallized how generative AI should integrate into structured work. Rather than unleashing models to be conversational and open-ended, anchoring them in a clear structure made them dramatically more useful. A framework isn't a constraint on AI—it's a design pattern that makes AI useful at scale.

Finally, this work reinforced that research isn't just validation—it shapes the direction. Every decision in the framework came from user feedback: the ordering of sections, the emphasis on open questions, the explicit ownership in next actions. The feature is stronger because research grounded it in real user needs.
